The Great Tit (Parus major)

A small passerine bird in the family Paridae

By Alejandra Pereira León
Great Tit, Photo by Petr Ganaj

The great tit, known for its distinct features including a black head and neck, prominent white cheeks, olive upperparts, and yellow underparts, exhibits slight variations across its numerous subspecies. Carl Linnaeus formally documented this bird under its current scientific name in the 1758 edition of Systema Naturae. It belongs to the tit family Paridae and is a small passerine bird. Found widely across Europe, the Middle East, Central Asia, and extending eastward across the Palearctic to the Amur River, and southward into parts of North Africa, it typically resides in various woodland habitats. Most great tits are sedentary, only migrating during severe winters.



Bird Perched on a Branch, Photo by David Selbert


The great tit, similar to other tit species, nests in cavities, typically within tree holes. According to the British Trust for Ornithology (BTO) (2023), They nest between April and June, usually producing one brood annually with clutches of up to nine eggs that are incubated for approximately 14 days. Their nests are vulnerable to raids by woodpeckers, squirrels, and weasels, and may also suffer from flea infestations.

Juveniles disperse from their parent's territory but typically do not travel far and the adults are preyed upon by sparrowhawks. The great tit has successfully adapted to human-altered environments and is a familiar sight in urban parks and gardens.

It generally does not migrate, with pairs typically remaining within or near their territories year-round, even in the northernmost parts of their range.

During the summer, it primarily feeds on insects and spiders gleaned from foliage, with larger invertebrate prey including cockroaches, grasshoppers, crickets, bees, wasps, snails, and woodlice. In winter, its diet expands to include a wider range of food items, such as small hibernating bats. And also the great tit is a significant species for ornithological studies due to it is wide distribution and adaptability to diverse habitats.

The great tit is widely distributed throughout Eurasia, spanning from Europe (excluding Iceland and northern Scandinavia) to Mediterranean islands, North Africa (Morocco, Algeria, Tunisia), the Middle East, and parts of Central and northern Asia.

It inhabits a variety of ecosystems including open deciduous woodland, mixed forests, forest edges, and gardens. In denser forests, it prefers clearings, while in Siberia it resides in boreal taiga. Across its range, it can also be found in oak forests, Atlas cedar stands, palm groves in North Africa, and riverine willow and birch forests in eastern Siberia, Mongolia, and China.

The Turkestan subspecies occupies riverine woodlands, low scrubland, and oases, adapting to habitats from dense forests to open areas with scattered trees at higher altitudes.



Acoording to the British Trust for Ornithology(BTO) (2023)Great tits typically live for around three years. However, the current longevity record for this species is 13 years and 11 months.

Similar to other tit species, is known for its vocalizations, possessing approximately 40 distinct types of calls and songs. While the calls are generally similar between males and females, males are significantly more vocal compared to females, who rarely call.
Soft single notes like "pit", "spick", or "chit" serve as contact calls. An emphatic "tink" is employed by adult males in alarming situations or during territorial disputes. One of its most recognizable calls is the "teacher, teacher" phrase, often described as resembling the sound of a squeaky wheelbarrow wheel, used to assert ownership of territory.

It is said amongst birders that if you do not recognise a bird call, it is likely to be a great tit.

There are currently 15 recognized subspecies of Great Tit ; some of them include:
  • P. m. Newtoni, described by Pražákin 1894, is found across the British Isles.
  • P. m Major, described by Linnaeus in 1758, is found throughout much of Europe, Asia Minor, northern and eastern Kazakhstan, southern Siberia and northern Mongolia, as far as the mid-Amur Valley.
  • P. m. Excelsus, described by Buvry in 1857, is found in northwestern Africa.
  • P. m. Corsus, described by Kleinschmidt in 1903, is found in Portugal, southern Spain, and Corsica.
  • P. m. Mallorcae, described by Von Jordans in 1913, is found in the Balearic Islands.
  • P. m. Ecki, described by Von Jordans in 1970, is found on Sardinia.

Males are distinguished by their vibrant plumage, featuring a black bib that runs down the belly, bordered by a lemon-yellow breast. The color of the male bird's chest has been associated with the quality of his sperm, being a way in which he demonstrates his reproductive superiority over females.

Higher levels of carotenoids intensify the yellow tone of the breast and strengthen sperm's ability to resist the damaging effects of free radicals. Females and juveniles look similar but have unexciting colors. In these birds, the black line down the belly is also narrower and sometimes discontinuous.
